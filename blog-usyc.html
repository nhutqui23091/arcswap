USYC, explained for stablecoin holders

A no-jargon walkthrough of how Hashnote's tokenized money market fund actually works — what backs it, how yield accrues, and what the real risks look like.

ArcSwap Team · Apr 27, 2026 · 8 min read

If you hold USDC and you're not earning yield on it, you're effectively giving Circle (the issuer) a free interest-rate loan. They invest your dollars in U.S. Treasury bills and pocket ~5%. USYC is the answer to that: a tokenized share of a regulated money market fund that passes the yield through to you, on-chain.

This post is the no-jargon version. By the end you should understand what USYC actually is, where the yield comes from, and what risks you take by holding it.

The 60-second version

  • USYC = U.S. Yield Coin, an ERC-20 token issued by Hashnote.
  • Each USYC represents one share of a regulated money market fund holding short-dated U.S. Treasury bills + overnight reverse repo agreements.
  • Yield ≈ ~4.85% APY (varies with the U.S. Federal Funds rate).
  • You don't get more tokens — instead the price per share (NAV) ticks up daily.
  • Redeem USYC for USDC any time during U.S. Treasury market hours.

Where the yield comes from

USYC's underlying portfolio is boring. That's the point.

Imagine the U.S. Treasury department needs to borrow money for 4-13 weeks. They issue a "T-bill" — a short-term IOU. You buy it at a small discount, and at maturity the Treasury pays you back the full face value. The difference is your yield.

Hashnote's fund holds a basket of these T-bills, plus some overnight repo (where the cash sits earning interest while waiting for the next bill purchase). As bills mature and roll, the interest accrues into the fund's Net Asset Value (NAV).

The NAV is just fund's total assets ÷ shares outstanding. Each USYC token is one share. As the underlying T-bills earn interest, NAV ticks up — that's your yield, mechanically.

In numbers
If you bought 1 USYC today at $1.1163 and the fund earns 4.85% over a year, in 12 months your 1 USYC is worth ~$1.1704. You still hold 1 token — the token price grew, not the count.

"Why don't I just buy T-bills directly?"

You can. But:

  • You need a U.S. brokerage account, KYC, ACH transfers, and a tax form.
  • Minimum is usually $100 per bill, $1,000 to be efficient.
  • You can't use it as collateral in DeFi.
  • You can't move it to another chain in 15 minutes.

USYC wraps all of that into a token that lives on-chain. Trade-off: you trust Hashnote (the fund manager) and the smart contracts. You're not buying T-bills directly; you're buying a tokenized claim on a fund that holds T-bills.

The "price per share, not rebase" detail

Two ways to deliver yield to a token holder:

  1. Rebase: token count goes up daily (like aUSDC, stETH). Your wallet shows more tokens, price per token stays at $1.
  2. Price-per-share: token count is constant, price per token grows. (USYC chose this.)

Why does USYC use price-per-share? Three reasons:

  • Composability: rebasing tokens break Uniswap and most DeFi integrations. Price-per-share works everywhere.
  • Tax accounting: easier to track cost basis when count is constant.
  • Industry norm: ERC-4626 vault standard uses price-per-share.

Trading hours matter

Crypto runs 24/7. U.S. Treasuries don't. The fund's NAV only updates during U.S. Treasury market hours: Monday–Friday, 09:30–16:00 ET, excluding U.S. bank holidays.

What this means in practice:

  • Outside those hours, your USYC balance is stable. The token still exists, you can still hold or transfer it.
  • Deposits and redemptions queue outside market hours and execute at the next NAV strike.
  • If you need instant exit at 3am Sunday, USYC isn't your tool — but USDC pools on Uniswap (which ArcSwap surfaces) are.

The risks, honestly

U.S. T-bills are the lowest-default-risk asset in global finance — but "lowest" isn't "zero". The actual risks layered onto USYC:

Read this not to scare you off, but so you know what you're holding. USYC is one of the safest yield-bearing assets in crypto. It's not safer than cash. There's no such thing.
