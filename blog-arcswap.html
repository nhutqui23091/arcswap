What is ArcSwap?

A yield terminal for stablecoins on Arc — USYC vault, native swap, CCTP bridge and liquidity pools, in one venue. Here's why we built it that way.

ArcSwap Team · Apr 27, 2026 · 6 min read

ArcSwap is a non-custodial DeFi frontend on the Arc Layer 1 blockchain. The pitch in one sentence: everything you want to do with stablecoins, in one tab.

That's vague. Let me be specific.

The problem we noticed

If you hold $10,000 USDC today and want to be productive with it, your day looks like this:

  • Open Aave to lend USDC for ~3% APY.
  • Open Uniswap to swap into EURC for an FX trade.
  • Open Stargate / Across to bridge to another chain.
  • Open USDM / sFRAX / sUSDe for yield-bearing stablecoin variants.
  • Open Etherscan three times to track approvals.

Five tabs, five wallet popups, five fee experiences, five mental models. And after all that, your USDC is fragmented across three protocols, two chains, and four token wrappers.

This is fine for crypto-native users who enjoy the friction. It's terrible for capital efficiency and even worse for new users.

The thesis: a yield terminal

What if your USDC just worked? Sitting in your wallet, earning U.S. Treasury yield by default, instantly swappable into other stablecoins, instantly bridgeable across chains, instantly LP-able into pools — all from a single interface, with one balance, one approval pattern, one mental model.

That's ArcSwap. Four products tightly integrated:

VAULT
USYC Yield
Earn ~4.85% APY on idle USDC via tokenized U.S. T-bills (Hashnote).
SWAP
In-Arc AMM
Convert USDC ↔ EURC ↔ USYC instantly via Uniswap V2.
BRIDGE
CCTP V2
Native USDC across chains. Burn-and-mint, no wrapped tokens.
POOLS
Liquidity Provision
Earn 0.25% per trade by providing USDC/EURC or USDC/USYC liquidity.

Why Arc, specifically

We picked Arc as the home chain because of three structural decisions Circle made when designing it:

  1. USDC as native gas. No more "I need to bridge in some ETH first to pay gas". Your USDC is the spending money and the gas. Predictable USD-denominated transaction costs.
  2. Sub-second deterministic finality. No reorgs, no "wait 12 blocks". Tx confirmed = tx done. Critical for cross-chain UX where the bridge waits for source-chain finality.
  3. EVM compatible. Solidity, Hardhat, Foundry, Viem all work. Hashnote already deployed USYC. Circle already deployed CCTP V2. Uniswap V2 router was deployed by the foundation. The infra was waiting.

We didn't have to build any of the protocols ArcSwap surfaces. They all exist on Arc. ArcSwap is a UX layer, not a protocol — and that's deliberate.

What we are NOT

To set expectations clearly:

  • We don't custody funds. Your wallet is your wallet. ArcSwap can't pause, freeze, or move anything you own.
  • We don't have our own token. No ASWP. No airdrop campaign. No governance vote. Anyone telling you otherwise is running a scam.
  • We don't have our own smart contracts. We call into Hashnote (USYC), Circle (CCTP), and the Arc Foundation's Uniswap deployment. They run the infra; we run the UI.
  • We don't take fees on yield, swaps, or bridges (current testnet stage). The fees you see come from the underlying protocols (Hashnote subscription fee, Uniswap LP fee, etc.).

Why this design choice

"Non-custodial frontend that doesn't run its own contracts" sounds boring. It is. Boring is a feature when real money is involved.

Look at the largest DeFi exploits of the last three years. The vast majority were not "the smart contract had a bug". They were:

  • Frontend supply-chain compromise (malicious JS pushed to a CDN)
  • Multi-sig key compromise (admin was a single hot wallet)
  • Bridge hack (custom messaging protocol with 1-of-N validators)

By not running our own contracts, we eliminate an entire class of risk — the kind that comes from rolling our own AMM math or our own bridge messaging. By being non-custodial, we eliminate the "founder runs off with the funds" risk. By using IPFS-pinnable static HTML, we minimize the supply-chain attack surface.

The cost: we're opinionated about what we surface. We pick USYC over a dozen alternatives. We pick CCTP over LayerZero. These choices simplify the UX but constrain it.

That's the trade we're making. Less optionality, less risk, less complexity. For users who want every yield-bearing stablecoin and every bridge in one place, there are aggregators. We're not that.
