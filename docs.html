ArcSwap Documentation

The engineering reference for the unified stablecoin terminal on Arc. ArcSwap unifies Circle's stablecoin stack — Gateway, CCTP V2, USDC, EURC — with Hashnote's tokenized U.S. Treasury yield (USYC) and a Uniswap V2 venue, all on the Arc Layer 1. This guide covers product flows, the on-chain surface, the proxy API, and the security baseline.

Introduction

ArcSwap is a non-custodial frontend that brings five stablecoin primitives together under a single UX on the Arc Layer 1:

Who is this for?

  • Stablecoin holders who want yield on idle USDC without leaving the dollar.
  • Multi-chain treasurers tired of fragmenting USDC into seven balances across seven chains.
  • Arc-native traders swapping inside the L1 with sub-second finality and predictable USD-denominated gas.
  • LPs earning fees on stablecoin and yield-bearing pairs.
  • Builders using ArcSwap as a reference implementation for Circle Gateway, CCTP V2, and USYC integrations.

Design principles

  • Custody never leaves you. ArcSwap holds no funds and has no admin path that can pause your access.
  • No build pipeline. The frontend is plain HTML/CSS/vanilla JS plus ethers.js — every served byte is reviewable, every CDN script is SRI-pinned.
  • Strict CSP, locked CDNs. Every page ships with a Content-Security-Policy. The only third-party origins reachable are the RPCs we explicitly trust.
  • Read paths through proxies. Reads to Circle's Gateway and IRIS APIs route through Cloudflare Pages Functions to keep the browser's connect-src tight and to dodge browser CORS quirks.

Architecture

ArcSwap is a thin frontend over three external protocol stacks. There is no ArcSwap-owned smart contract and no centralized backend that can pause user actions. What lives on our origin is static HTML, a few KB of shared JavaScript, and two stateless proxy routes.

What "non-custodial" means in practice

  • Every state-changing action is a transaction signed by your wallet against a third-party contract (Hashnote, Circle, or Uniswap-on-Arc).
  • ArcSwap has no privileged role on any of these contracts: no owner, no admin, no upgrade path. Read the verified source on the Arc explorer.
  • The proxy routes (/api/*) only reformulate read requests to Circle's public APIs. They cannot move funds, sign messages, or hold state.

Quick start

From a fresh browser to your first vault deposit in five steps.

  1. Install a wallet. MetaMask, Rabby, Coinbase Wallet, Frame, or any other EIP-6963-compliant browser wallet. See Supported wallets.
  2. Add Arc Testnet. Open arcswap.net/trade, click Connect, and approve the network prompt. Manual params are in the Network info table below.
  3. Get testnet USDC. Use the Circle faucet on your address. Arc uses USDC as native gas, so one faucet drip covers both spending and gas fees.
  4. Open the Vault. Go to arcswap.net/vault, enter a USDC amount, click Deposit. First deposit is two transactions (approve + deposit), subsequent deposits are one.
  5. Watch yield accrue. Your USYC balance reflects the live NAV of the underlying T-bill fund. Yield posts each U.S. Treasury market session (Mon–Fri 09:30–16:00 ET, ex U.S. bank holidays).
Tip
If you hold USDC on a different chain (Sepolia, Base, Arbitrum, etc.), skip the faucet and use Unified Balance instead — deposit your existing USDC into the Gateway and it becomes spendable on Arc immediately.

Wallet setup

ArcSwap uses EIP-6963 for wallet discovery (the multi-injector standard that replaced the old window.ethereum singleton). Any wallet that announces itself via eip6963:announceProvider appears in our wallet picker, ranked by RDNS so the choice is deterministic when multiple wallets are installed.

Once connected, the session is held entirely in browser memory. There is no auth cookie, no JWT, no session token issued by ArcSwap.

Unified Balance

The /balance page is the centerpiece of ArcSwap. It uses Circle Gateway V1 to give you a single USDC balance that's spendable on every supported chain — no bridge clicks, no chain switching, no wrapped tokens.

How it works

Each Gateway-supported chain has a GatewayWallet contract at the same deterministic address. You deposit USDC into the wallet on whichever chain you currently hold it. Circle's Gateway service issues attestations that authorize a fresh mint of native USDC on the destination chain — so the USDC you spend is always native USDC, not a wrapped derivative.

Deposit flow

  1. Select a source chain with a USDC balance (Sepolia, Base, Arbitrum, OP, Polygon Amoy, Fuji, Unichain).
  2. Approve the GatewayWallet to pull USDC (one-time per chain per token).
  3. Deposit — your USDC moves from your wallet into the GatewayWallet contract on that chain.
  4. Done. Your unified balance updates within seconds. You can now spend it on Arc (or any other Gateway chain) without further bridging.

Spend flow (mint on Arc)

  1. Submit an EIP-712 signed burn intent to Circle's Gateway service.
  2. Gateway validates available balance across all your deposits, signs an attestation.
  3. Your wallet calls GatewayMinter.gatewayMint(attestation, signature) on the destination chain (Arc).
  4. Native USDC appears on Arc, ready to use in the Vault, Swap, or Pool.
Why this is different from a normal bridge
A traditional bridge holds your USDC in a custodian contract and gives you a wrapped IOU. Gateway burns and re-issues native USDC attested by Circle. There's no bridge TVL to drain, no wrapped derivative to unwrap, and the USDC you spend is the same USDC accepted everywhere else.

Direct deposits on Arc

Arc's USDC is the native gas token, which lives in the L1 ledger rather than in an ERC-20 wrapper. GatewayWallet.deposit relies on standard approve/transferFrom, which can only move ERC-20 balances, so direct deposits from Arc are disabled. To fund Arc, deposit from any other supported chain and let the Gateway mint native USDC on Arc.

USYC Vault

The USYC Vault wraps Hashnote's U.S. Yield Coin (USYC), a tokenized money-market fund backed 1:1 by short-duration U.S. Treasury bills and overnight reverse-repurchase agreements. Depositing USDC into the vault calls Hashnote's Teller contract, which mints USYC at the live navigator-published NAV. Redeeming USYC burns it for USDC at the same NAV.

How the yield works

  • The fund holds short-dated T-bills (typically 1-month and 3-month tenors).
  • As bills mature and roll, interest accrues to the fund's NAV.
  • USYC's price-per-share rises by approximately the daily yield each U.S. business day.
  • Currently targeting ~4.85% APY, varying with the U.S. Treasury rate environment.

Depositing

  1. Connect wallet, ensure you're on Arc Testnet with a USDC balance.
  2. Open /vault, enter the USDC amount.
  3. First deposit triggers an approve transaction (one-time per token, gives the Teller permission to pull USDC).
  4. Second transaction is the deposit itself — you receive USYC at the NAV-priced share count.

Redeeming

Redemptions are processed at the next NAV strike during U.S. Treasury market hours. Outside hours, the request queues and settles when the market opens.

Smart contract surface
The Vault calls into the Hashnote USYC Teller at 0x9fdF14c5B14173D74C08Af27AebFf39240dC105A. ArcSwap is a frontend; the underlying contract is operated by Hashnote.

Swap (Arc internal)

The swap module on the /trade page routes through Uniswap V2 deployed by the Arc Foundation. It supports direct and 2-hop paths between USDC, EURC, and USYC. Slippage default is 0.5% (configurable up to a hard cap of 5%).

Quote pipeline

  1. The router contract is queried via getAmountsOut(amountIn, path) for every viable path.
  2. The frontend picks the path with the highest output, factoring in pool reserves and the 0.25% Arc-deploy fee.
  3. You approve the input token (one-time per token), then call swapExactTokensForTokens with your minOut.

Pairs currently surfaced

  • USDC / EURC — stablecoin FX pair
  • USDC / USYC — cash vs. yield-bearing pair
  • EURC / USYC — via 2-hop

CCTP Bridge

The bridge module on /trade uses CCTP V2 (Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol) — Circle's burn-and-mint mechanism. Native USDC is destroyed on the source chain, Circle's IRIS service issues a signed attestation, and the same USDC is freshly minted on the destination chain. No wrapped tokens, no liquidity pools, no bridge custodian.

End-to-end flow

  1. Choose source chain and destination chain (any pair from the 8 supported).
  2. Enter USDC amount and recipient address (defaults to your own).
  3. Burn tx on source — calls TokenMessengerV2.depositForBurn(amount, destDomain, recipient, burnToken, ...).
  4. Wait for Circle's IRIS attestation (typically 10–20 minutes on testnet, 13–19 minutes on mainnet — Circle waits for source-chain finality).
  5. Mint tx on destination — calls MessageTransmitterV2.receiveMessage(message, attestation). ArcSwap submits this for you when the attestation arrives.

Resumability

A burn tx hash is the only thing needed to mint on the destination later. If you close the tab mid-flight, return to /trade with the same wallet and the pending mint will appear automatically. Or use Circle's reference dashboard or any other CCTP-aware tool to redeem the attestation manually.

Latency is dominated by the slowest chain
Arc has sub-second deterministic finality, so an Arc → other-chain bridge waits only on the destination's finality clock. An Ethereum Sepolia → Arc bridge waits 13+ minutes for two beacon-chain epochs of finality before Circle issues the attestation.

Liquidity Pools

The /pool page lets you provide liquidity to Uniswap V2 pairs on Arc and earn the pool's share of swap fees (the Arc deploy uses a 0.25% per-trade fee, of which the LP earns the full amount).

Adding liquidity

  1. Pick a pair, enter amounts (the second amount auto-fills based on the current pool ratio).
  2. Approve both tokens (one-time per token, per spender).
  3. Click Add Liquidity — you receive LP tokens representing your share of the pool.

Removing liquidity

Burn your LP tokens to redeem the underlying tokens at the current pool ratio. Accumulated fees are baked into the redeemed amounts (Uniswap V2's "k-grows" model).

Impermanent loss — yes, even on stablecoins
USDC/EURC drifts with the EUR/USD FX rate. USDC/USYC drifts as USYC's NAV accrues. Your withdrawn token amounts will differ from what you deposited. Always track total value, not token quantities.

Activity Points

The /point page tabulates your activity across vault deposits, swaps, bridges, and LP positions. Points are non-transferable and have no monetary value during testnet — they exist solely as an indicator of engagement and may inform future incentive programs.

Points are computed client-side from on-chain events visible to the connected wallet. There is no off-chain database storing your activity.

Arc L1

Arc is a purpose-built Layer 1 blockchain optimized for stablecoin-denominated finance. Three defining design choices set it apart from a generic EVM chain:

  • USDC-denominated gas. Transaction fees are paid in USDC, so costs are predictable in USD terms regardless of any token's price action.
  • Sub-second deterministic finality. No reorgs, no probabilistic finality. Once a transaction is finalized, it cannot revert.
  • Opt-in privacy. Confidential transfers and selective disclosure for regulated counterparties (not yet integrated by ArcSwap).

Arc is fully EVM-compatible: existing Solidity contracts, Hardhat / Foundry / Viem / ethers tooling all work without modification. Reference docs at docs.arc.network.

Risk surface

  • Issuer risk. Hashnote operates the fund. Their compliance, custody, and operational integrity matter.
  • Smart-contract risk. The USYC token and Teller contracts have been audited; no audit eliminates risk.
  • Market hours. Redemptions queue outside U.S. Treasury market hours — you cannot exit instantly during a panic.
  • Yield variability. The headline APY tracks short-term U.S. Treasury rates and moves with the federal funds rate.

Circle Gateway

Circle Gateway is the protocol that powers Unified Balance. It replaces "I bridged my USDC to chain X and now I have to bridge it back" with "my USDC is already everywhere I need it."

The two on-chain contracts

  • GatewayWallet at 0x0077777d7EBA4688BDeF3E311b846F25870A19B9 on every Gateway chain. You deposit USDC into this contract; balances are held per-chain but indexed globally.
  • GatewayMinter at 0x0022222ABE238Cc2C7Bb1f21003F0a260052475B on every Gateway chain. Anyone with a valid Circle-signed attestation can call gatewayMint to materialize USDC on the destination chain.

Both addresses are deterministic across chains (CREATE2 deploy with the same salt), which is why Unified Balance can address every chain uniformly without a per-chain registry.

CCTP V2

Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol V2 is Circle's official bridge for native USDC. Unlike lock-and-mint bridges (which create wrapped tokens backed by frozen liquidity), CCTP burns native USDC on the source chain and mints native USDC on the destination — every USDC stays "real USDC" all the way through.

Why this matters

  • No wrapped USDC variants. No USDC.e, no axlUSDC, no synthetic IOU.
  • No bridge TVL to drain — there's no pool of locked tokens for an attacker to target.
  • Issuer-attested. Only Circle's signed attestation can authorize the destination mint.

Trade-offs vs. Gateway

  • Latency. CCTP waits for source-chain finality before issuing the attestation. Gateway uses Circle's centralized service to attest immediately against on-chain deposits.
  • Two transactions on the user side. Burn on source, receive on destination — both paid by the user.
  • Limited chain coverage. Both protocols only support chains Circle has activated. ArcSwap surfaces all currently-supported testnets.

USDC as native gas

Most chains use a volatile native token for gas (ETH, MATIC, AVAX). Arc uses USDC. The practical impact:

  • Gas costs are predictable in USD terms — a transfer that costs $0.001 today costs $0.001 tomorrow, regardless of any token's price action.
  • You don't need to maintain a "gas reserve" in a separate token. The same USDC you trade with pays for the trade.
  • Internally, native USDC has 18 decimals (to match standard EVM gas math), distinct from the canonical 6-decimal Circle USDC. The frontend handles the conversion at the burn/mint boundary using a cctpDecimals hint on the token registry.

Implementation note

Arc's USDC lives at 0x3600000000000000000000000000000000000000 as a precompile-style ledger token, not a deployed ERC-20. Standard balanceOf/transfer/ approve all work, but the token cannot be deposited via transferFrom to any contract that wasn't designed for native gas — which is why Gateway deposits originate from other chains.

API endpoints

ArcSwap exposes two read-only proxy routes implemented as Cloudflare Pages Functions. They exist to keep the browser's connect-src CSP allowlist tight (one origin per upstream Circle service) and to avoid CORS preflight issues. Neither route holds state, signs anything, or moves funds.

/api/circle-proxy/*

Proxies Circle's IRIS attestation API for CCTP. Used by the bridge module to poll for attestations after a burn transaction. 

# Fetch attestation by burn message hash
GET /api/circle-proxy/v2/messages/{sourceDomain}?transactionHash={txHash}
   ↓ proxies to https://iris-api-sandbox.circle.com/v2/messages/...

/api/gateway-proxy/*

Proxies Circle's Gateway public REST API. Used by the Unified Balance module to fetch balances and submit signed transfer intents. 

# Read multi-chain balance
POST /api/gateway-proxy/v1/balances
     # body: { token, sources: [{ domain, depositor }] }

# Submit a signed burn-intent for cross-chain spend
POST /api/gateway-proxy/v1/transfer
     # body: signed EIP-712 BurnIntent
No API key on the client
Circle's Gateway API requires a server-issued bearer token. The Pages Function injects it server-side; the browser never sees it. The token is scoped to read-only and intent-submission endpoints — it cannot move user funds.

Tech stack

Deliberately minimal. Every choice was made to maximize auditability and minimize attack surface.

Frontend
Plain HTML + CSS + vanilla JavaScript. No framework, no JSX, no transpiler, no build step.
On-chain library
ethers.js v6 via SRI-pinned UMD CDN — the only runtime dependency.
Hosting
Cloudflare Pages — free tier, edge CDN, DDoS protection, automatic HTTPS.
Functions
Cloudflare Pages Functions for the two proxy routes. No persistent backend.
Optional path
IPFS pin + ENS contenthash for an immutable, decentralized backup deploy.
Source
~14 HTML files + four shared JS modules (~1,900 LOC). First paint < 1s on 4G.

Why no framework?

  • Auditability. Every line shipped to your browser is in the repo as written, not generated by a bundler. You can diff the served HTML against the GitHub source byte-for-byte.
  • SRI integrity. The only third-party script (ethers.js) is pinned by SHA-384. Any tamper at the CDN fails to load instead of executing.
  • Performance. No runtime, no hydration. The vault page is interactive in < 200 ms after first byte.
  • Footprint. No node_modules, no supply-chain blast radius from npm install, no dependency CVEs.

Security

ArcSwap takes a defense-in-depth approach. Highlights:

  • Non-custodial. No funds ever flow through ArcSwap-controlled addresses.
  • Open source. The entire frontend is published. Verify the served build matches the committed source.
  • Strict CSP. A Content-Security-Policy header constrains script, style, image, font, and connect origins per page. There is no 'unsafe-eval'.
  • SRI on every CDN script. ethers.js (and any other CDN asset) carries a SHA-384 integrity hash; CDN tampering fails closed.
  • Host security headers. HSTS, X-Frame-Options, X-Content-Type-Options, Referrer-Policy, Permissions-Policy — all set via Cloudflare Pages _headers.
  • No third-party tracking. No analytics, no fingerprinting scripts, no marketing pixels. The only third-party origins reachable are the chain RPCs and Circle's APIs explicitly allowlisted in the CSP.
  • IPFS + ENS (optional). Production builds are content-addressed and pinned to multiple providers; ENS contenthash updates require a multi-sig signature.
  • Pre-flight checks. Every deploy runs scripts/preflight-check.sh — six gates covering CSP, SRI, secrets hygiene, redirects, and host headers.

Found a vulnerability? Report it via SECURITY.md — bug-bounty rewards from $100 (low) to $50,000 (critical), depending on impact. Coordinated disclosure expected.

FAQ

Why does USYC sometimes show 0% accrual?

U.S. Treasury markets are closed on weekends and U.S. bank holidays. During those windows the fund's NAV is fixed and your token quantity is unchanged, so the displayed APY rounds to zero. Yield resumes on the next U.S. business day.

Why is my CCTP bridge taking 20 minutes?

Circle waits for source-chain finality before issuing the attestation that authorizes the destination mint. On Arc this is sub-second; on Ethereum Sepolia it can take 13+ minutes (waiting for two epochs of beacon-chain finality). Total time is dominated by the slowest chain involved.

When should I use Unified Balance vs. CCTP Bridge?

Use Unified Balance if you want USDC ready to spend on multiple chains over time. Deposit once, spend many times — the latency is in the seconds. Use the CCTP Bridge for a one-shot move from chain A to chain B where you don't need to pre-fund.

Can I lose money in the USYC vault?

In theory, yes — money market funds can technically "break the buck" if their underlying assets default. In practice, U.S. Treasury bills are the lowest-default-risk asset in global finance. Specific risks are detailed in the USYC section.

Does ArcSwap have its own token?

No. ArcSwap is a frontend, not a protocol. There is no ASWP token, no airdrop campaign, no governance token sale. Anyone telling you otherwise is running a scam.

When mainnet?

Arc mainnet is governed by the Arc Foundation; ArcSwap deploys on mainnet shortly after Arc launches. See the homepage roadmap for current milestones.

Why don't you use a bigger frontend framework?

We optimized for auditability and minimum supply-chain risk. See Tech stack.
